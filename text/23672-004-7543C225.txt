Roscoe Conkling,  (born Oct. 30, 1829, Albany, N.Y., U.S.â€”died  April 18, 1888, New York City) prominent U.S. Republican leader in the post-Civil War period. He was known for his support of severe Reconstruction measures toward the South and his insistence on the control of political patronage in his home state of New York.
Admitted to the bar in 1850, Conkling soon established a reputation as a lawyer, orator, and Whig Party leader. In 1858 he ran as a Republican and was elected to the U.S. House of Representatives, serving for six years. In 1867 he won a seat in the Senate, where he held office for 14 years.
